Background

From Choice Blindness to LLM refusal bypass

The project is inspired by the BBC's Choice Blindness experiments, where participants failed to notice when their own choices were swapped. Translated to LLMs: if a model is shown the start of an answer it supposedly gave to a forbidden query, will it complete that answer as if it were its own?

Modern aligned LLMs are trained to refuse at the beginning of a harmful response — they learn the distribution of openings like "I can't help with that" or "I'm sorry, but…". Once past that refusal opening, the alignment signal fades: next-token prediction takes over and the model continues whatever pattern it sees. Prefix injection exploits exactly that asymmetry by removing the decision point where refusal is most likely to fire.

Method

How prefix injection bypasses alignment

The attacker crafts a conversation where the assistant's prior turn begins with an affirmative prefix — for example, the opening words of a compliant answer to a harmful request. The target model, anchored by the fabricated prefix, continues the completion rather than refusing. No fine-tuning, no gradient access — just prompt construction.

Concretely, the attack has three moving parts: (1) a harmful user request, (2) a fabricated assistant turn whose opening words commit to answering it, and (3) a continuation request. Because the model's context window already contains its own "compliant" opening, generating a refusal would require contradicting itself — an option rarely selected at inference time.

Results

Attack success across models

Tested against Gemini 2.5 Pro and DeepSeek R1 on the MaliciousInstruct benchmark. Baseline refusal rate is near 100%. With prefix injection applied, attack success reaches near 100% — effectively nullifying the models' safety alignment on this benchmark.

Gemini 2.5 Pro ~0% ~100% +100 pp

ASR = Attack Success Rate on the MaliciousInstruct benchmark. "~0% / ~100%" reflects the empirical pattern observed across prompts: baseline refusals are near-universal, attacked completions are near-universal.

Related work

How this fits into the jailbreak literature

Prefix injection sits in a broader family of prompt-level jailbreaks that don't require model access. Three lines of prior work are especially relevant:

  • Wei, Haghtalab, Steinhardt — Jailbroken: How Does LLM Safety Training Fail? Formalizes "competing objectives" and "mismatched generalization" as root causes — the framework this work slots into.
  • Zou, Wang, Kolter, Fredrikson — Universal and Transferable Adversarial Attacks on Aligned Language Models (GCG). Achieves similar bypasses via gradient-optimized suffixes; prefix injection gets comparable ASR without any gradient access.
  • Huang et al. — Catastrophic Jailbreak of Open-source LLMs via Exploiting Generation. Source of the MaliciousInstruct benchmark used here.
Limitations

What these numbers don't say

A few caveats worth stating explicitly. (1) MaliciousInstruct is a curated red-team set; production distributions look different. (2) "Success" is measured as a non-refusal completion, not by downstream harm potential — many completions are plausible-sounding but operationally useless. (3) The attack depends on the provider exposing a multi-turn API where assistant turns can be specified by the caller; providers can harden by verifying assistant-turn provenance. (4) Results were collected at a specific point in time against specific model versions; both providers ship alignment updates frequently, so exact numbers will drift.

Responsible disclosure

Why publish this at all

This is defensive security research. The repository documents the attack so that alignment teams can harden against it. Findings are aimed at helping model providers close this failure mode, not at enabling real-world misuse.

The repository contains abstract attack scaffolding; concrete payload prompts are redacted or gated. The providers whose models are named have published guidance inviting this class of research and accept vulnerability reports through standard channels.
